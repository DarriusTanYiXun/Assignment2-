Does where you live shape the schools and student communities around you?

Australian schools are not evenly distributed across the country. Some students live in places with many nearby schools and several sectors to choose from, including government, Catholic and independent schools. Other students live in areas where options are more limited. A student's socioeconomic background can also affect the school sectors available to them and shape their school experience.

Where are schools available?

Knowing where schools are located and how many schools are nearby is an important part of understanding school access. States and regions do not have the same number or distribution of schools. The availability of different school sectors also affects the choices families can make.

Schools cluster around major cities and along the east coast. More students attend schools in these areas than in regional and remote areas. Schools in regional and remote areas are fewer and spread across larger distances. Students in dense urban areas are therefore more likely to have several nearby school options, while students in less populated areas may have more limited access and choice.

These maps show where schools exist, but not all schools are the same. Australia has three distinct school sectors. They are government, Catholic and independent schools. The school sector a child attends shapes much of what follows.

What school choices are available?

Understanding the national sector breakdown, the distribution of sectors across remoteness areas, the types of schools in each state and the variation in students per teacher is important for families making school choice decisions.

Government schools make up the largest share of Australian schools. Catholic and independent schools form smaller but still important parts of the system. The sectors are not equally represented nationally, which affects the range of options available to families.

School sector choice by remoteness

Most schools in each sector are located in major cities. Regional and remote areas contain smaller shares of schools. Government schools are more widely represented outside major cities than Catholic and independent schools. Families seeking Catholic or independent schools may therefore have fewer options in regional and remote areas, while government schools are available across a broader range of locations.

The national picture masks significant variation between states. New South Wales and Victoria have the largest numbers of independent schools, while the Northern Territory is almost entirely served by government schools. Where a family lives shapes which sectors are available.

Families do not always have the same school choices. The type of school they choose depends on their child's age, needs and nearby options. Larger states have more schools overall, but each state has a different mix of primary, secondary, combined and special schools. Some families may find it harder to access the type of school that best suits their child because of where they live.

Beyond what types of schools exist, there are also differences in what resources those schools have. One of the most direct measures is how many students share each teacher. This varies significantly across sectors and locations.

At first, very remote areas appear to have fewer students per teacher. However, this does not automatically mean that very remote schools are better resourced. Many remote schools are small, so the ratio of students to teachers can look low even when the total number of teachers is much smaller. The bar chart on the right provides important context. Most teaching staff are concentrated in major cities, where most schools and students are located. Remote schools may have fewer students per teacher, but they also operate with a much smaller teaching workforce.

School systems also differ by scale. A school with 6,000 students offers a very different experience from a school with 300 students through its facilities, subject choices and sense of community.

So far, we have looked at the structure of the school system. This includes where schools are located, what choices exist, how resources vary and how large schools can be. But structure is only the first part of the story. The next question is who different schools actually serve.

Who do different schools serve?

To understand school experiences, we need to look beyond school numbers and resources. Different schools serve different student communities, and these differences can shape their learning environments. Terms such as ICSEA, SEA quarters, CRICOS registration, LBOTE and Indigenous enrolments help us explore student backgrounds.

Socio educational advantage (ICSEA) distribution across school sectors

Higher ICSEA = more advantaged community

Lower ICSEA = more disadvantaged community

The width of each violin shows where schools are concentrated. Higher ICSEA scores indicate that students generally come from more advantaged backgrounds. Catholic and independent schools cluster above 1,000, with almost no schools below 800. Government schools have a wider spread, with many schools below 1,000 and some above 1,000. This means Catholic and independent schools tend to serve more advantaged communities, while government schools serve a broader range of communities, including more disadvantaged ones.

Community advantage is not only a school sector pattern. It is also geographic. Brushing across ICSEA scores shows where schools serving less advantaged communities are concentrated and how the mix of sectors changes across Australia.

That advantage pattern becomes important when looking at overseas student access. The schools available to international students require CRICOS registration.

Schools open to overseas students are concentrated in Australia's more advantaged communities

CRICOS stands for the Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students. A school requires CRICOS registration to enrol overseas students.

Across all three sectors, schools with CRICOS registration sit noticeably higher on the community advantage scale than schools without registration. The gap is largest among government schools, where the median registered school sits 28 percentile points above the median school without registration. This means overseas families considering public schooling can only choose from government schools in wealthier areas. Independent schools with CRICOS registration sit even higher, with a median at the 92nd percentile. Across every sector, the schools available to overseas families represent the more advantaged end of Australian communities.

Student backgrounds are not evenly distributed across sectors. Government schools have the largest share of students from the bottom SEA quarter, while independent schools have the largest share from the top SEA quarter. Catholic schools sit between the two, with a more mixed student profile. Students from more disadvantaged backgrounds are therefore more likely to attend government schools, while students from more advantaged backgrounds are more likely to attend independent schools.

Socio educational background is one lens. Language background is another. Schools with higher proportions of students from homes where English is not the main language tell a different story about where disadvantage is concentrated and which sector serves these students.

Unlike socioeconomic background, language background does not neatly follow the advantage scale. Schools with high proportions of students from homes where English is not the main language exist at every ICSEA level. A child's language background alone does not predict which sector they will attend. Government schools are present across the entire range and serve Australia's linguistic diversity at every level of advantage. Catholic and independent schools serve a narrower and more advantaged group.

The previous charts looked at socio educational advantage and language background separately. This chart brings them together by showing how student enrolments move from school sector, to ICSEA band, and then to broad language background groups.

How sector, advantage and language background connect

Each flow represents student enrolments. Wider flows show pathways followed by more students from each school sector through each ICSEA score band and into a broad language background category.

Of the 199,372 students in schools below ICSEA 900, the most disadvantaged communities in Australia, almost all attend government schools. Catholic and independent schools are nearly absent from this band entirely. At the other end, the 1,100+ band contains a far higher mix of Catholic and independent students. Language background remains varied at every advantage level, reinforcing the earlier finding that linguistic diversity and socio-economic disadvantage operate independently.

In the Northern Territory, nearly 44% of government school students are Indigenous, the highest proportion of any state and sector combination in Australia. Government schools have the largest share of Indigenous students in every state and territory. This reflects both the geographic concentration of Indigenous communities and the limited availability of Catholic and independent schools in many remote areas. For Indigenous families in remote Australia, government schooling is often the only nearby option.

Taken together, these patterns return to the question raised at the start. In Australia, where students live and the socio-educational background of their community are strongly connected to the schools available around them, the resources those schools have, and the students they learn alongside. The three school sectors do not serve identical groups of students. Government schools cover the widest range of communities, including many regional, remote and lower-ICSEA areas, while Catholic and Independent schools are more concentrated in higher-advantage communities.
