Does where you live and what school you go to shape your education in Australia?

Australian schools are not spread evenly across the country. Some students live in places with many nearby schools and several sectors (Government, Catholic or Independent) to choose from, while others live in areas where options are more limited.

Australian school access and school experience are shaped by geography. Major city students usually have more nearby schools and more sector choice, while remote students may face fewer and more spread-out options. School sector is also linked to differences in socio-educational advantage and student background.

Section 1

Where are schools available?

School choice is not the same everywhere. The number of schools parents can choose for their children depends on where they live, especially which state or region they are in. If schools are close by, getting to school may be easier. However, students may need to spend more time commuting if schools are spread far apart.

This is important because the time spent commuting can affect everyday life. A longer commute can reduce the time students have for studying, resting, spending time with family, or doing other activities. This section looks at where schools are located across Australia and which areas have fewer accessible school options.

Where Australian schools are located

Each point is a school. Larger points show schools with more enrolments. Colour shows the school sector: Government, Catholic or Independent.

This shows schools clustered around major cities and the east coast, while large inland and remote areas have fewer visible school points.

Where are schools most concentrated?

Colour shows schools per square kilometre in each SA3 region. Darker regions have more schools packed into each square kilometre.

This shows where school options are more concentrated. Higher values suggest more nearby school options within a smaller area. Students in these areas may have more nearby school choices.

Section summary: Schools are concentrated in major cities and selected coastal regions. This means students in dense urban areas are likely to have more nearby school options than students in sparsely populated regional and remote areas.

Section 2

What school choices are available?

Having a school nearby is only one part of access. Families may also care about sector, school type, location and teaching resources. This section shows how those choices differ across Australia.

What types of school sectors make up Australia's school system?

This donut chart shows the national breakdown of schools by sector. The three sectors are Government, Catholic and Independent.

Why this matters: this chart gives the starting point for school choice. Government schools make up the largest share of Australian schools, while Catholic and Independent schools form smaller but still important parts of the system.

What types of schools are found in each state?

This stacked bar chart compares school types across states and territories. School type refers to whether a school is Primary, Secondary, Combined or Special.

Why this matters: the school options available to students are not identical across states. Larger states have more schools overall, but the mix of primary, secondary, combined and special schools also changes.

Are school sectors spread differently across remote and city areas?

This heatmap shows where each school sector is located across remoteness areas. Darker cells mean a larger share of that sector's schools are in that type of area.

Why this matters: school sector choice changes by location. A sector that is common in major cities may be less common in remote areas, which can limit the kinds of schools families can realistically choose.

How many students are there per teacher across sectors and locations?

This connected dot plot compares students per full-time-equivalent (FTE) teaching staff member across school sectors and remoteness areas.

Why this matters: access is not only about school numbers. Student-teacher ratios add a resourcing layer, although they do not directly measure class size or school quality.

Section summary: School choice changes by place. The balance of Government, Catholic and Independent schools, the mix of school types and student-teacher ratios all vary across states and remoteness areas.

Section 3

Who do different schools serve?

The next question is whether different kinds of schools serve similar student communities. This section uses Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage (ICSEA), SEA quarters, Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students (CRICOS) registration, LBOTE and Indigenous enrolment to look at school-community advantage and student background.

Do school sectors differ in socio-educational advantage?

This box plot compares Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage (ICSEA) scores across Government, Catholic and Independent schools. Higher values indicate greater socio-educational advantage in the school community.

  • Sector comparison: Catholic and Independent schools generally have higher ICSEA scores, indicating greater socio-educational advantage within their school communities compared to Government schools.
  • Government sector: This sector shows the widest variation in scores, with many low-outlier points representing schools serving communities with significantly lower socio-educational advantage.
  • Understanding the plot:
    • Coloured box: represents the middle 50% of schools in that sector.
    • Horizontal line: indicates the median score, or typical school, for the sector.
    • Small circles: represent outlier schools with unusually high or low scores compared to the rest of their sector.

Why this matters: in this chart, Catholic and Independent schools tend to sit higher than Government schools on the ICSEA scale. The low outlier points also show that some schools serve much lower-advantage communities, especially within the Government sector.

Are schools approved through the Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students (CRICOS) more advantaged?

Each dot is a school. This chart compares schools matched to Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students (CRICOS) locations with other schools, using Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage (ICSEA) percentile as a measure of school-community advantage.

Why this matters: Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students (CRICOS) registration means a school or provider location is approved to offer education to overseas students. This does not show overseas student counts, but it shows whether approved schools tend to sit higher or lower on the advantage scale.

Do school sectors serve different socio-educational groups?

Each bar adds up to 100%. It shows the estimated share of students from the bottom, lower-middle, upper-middle and top SEA quarters within each school sector.

Why this matters: this chart uses each school's SEA quarter percentages and total enrolments to estimate the student mix within each sector. It shows whether sectors serve different socio-educational groups.

How does Language Background Other Than English vary with the Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage?

This scatter plot compares each school's ICSEA score with the percentage of students recorded as having Language Background Other Than English. Each point represents one school.

Why this matters: LBOTE is one way to view language background diversity. The trend line suggests only a weak relationship, so it should not be read as a strong or causal pattern.

Where are Indigenous enrolments highest?

This grouped bar chart compares the share of Indigenous enrolments across states and school sectors. Percentages are weighted by total enrolments.

Why this matters: this chart adds Australian context by showing that student background differs by both state and school sector. It links place, sector and community background in one view.

Section summary: Different school sectors serve different student communities. The charts show variation in socio-educational advantage, SEA quarter mix, language background, Indigenous enrolment and CRICOS registration.

Section 4

How big are the largest schools?

The final part of the dashboard adds school size. Size does not mean quality, but it can affect the school experience because larger schools may have different facilities, subject choices, staffing needs and community structures.

Which schools have the largest enrolments?

This treemap shows the largest Australian schools by total enrolments in the ACARA School Profile 2025 dataset, grouped by school sector.

Larger rectangles represent larger enrolments. Labels appear only where the full school name fits; hover over each rectangle for full details. Why this matters: school size adds another way that the school experience can differ, because some students attend very large schools while others do not.

Section summary: School size adds another layer to the story. The largest schools are not evenly distributed across sectors, and enrolment scale can shape the schooling experience for students.

Final conclusion: this dashboard does not prove direct cause and effect. It cannot say that location or school sector alone causes a student's educational experience. However, it does show that geography and school type are connected to school access, school choice, socio-educational advantage and student background in Australia.

Data sources: ACARA School Location 2025; ACARA School Profile 2025; Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students (CRICOS) locations data; ABS remoteness categories included in the ACARA location data; and ABS ASGS Edition 3 SA3 2021 digital boundary files.
