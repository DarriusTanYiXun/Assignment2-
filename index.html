Does where you live shape the schools and student communities around you?

Australian schools are not evenly spread across the country. Some students live in places with many nearby schools and several sectors to choose from such as Government, Catholic or Independent, while others live in areas where options are more limited. Students socio-educational advantage and student background also plays a part in which school sector they can choose from and how this may impact their schooling expereience.

Where are schools available?

Knowing where schools are located and how many schools are nearby is an important part of understanding school access. This is important because not all states and regions have the same number of schools and spread distribution. Even more so with the school sectors, impacting their school choice.

Families do not always have the same school choices. The type of school they choose depends on their child’s age, needs and what is available nearby. Larger states have more schools overall, but each state has a different mix of primary, secondary, combined and special schools. This means some families may find it harder than others to access the type of school that best suits their child depending on where they live.

Beyond what types of schools exist, there are also differences in what resources those schools have. One of the most direct measures is how many students share each teacher - a gap that plays out differently across sectors and locations.

The students in government, Catholic and independent schools do not reflect a random cross-section of Australian society - the differences are stark, and they start with the communities each sector is built into.

Who do different schools serve?

Knowing your school-community advantage and student background is important for understanding the school experience. This is because different schools may serve different communities and student populations, which can affect the resources, opportunities and support available to students. The next question is whether different kinds of schools serve similar student communities. This section uses Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage (ICSEA), SEA quarters, Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students (CRICOS) registration, LBOTE and Indigenous enrolment to look at school-community advantage and student background.

Socio-educational advantage (ICSEA) distribution across school sectors

Higher ICSEA = more advantaged community

This shows that wider parts of each violin are where more schools are clustered and higher ICSEA means students at that school generally come from more advantaged backgrounds. While Catholic and Independent schools cluster tightly above 1,000, with almost no presence below 800. Government schools have a wider spread, with many schools below 1,000 and some above 1,000. This means that Catholic and Independent schools tend to serve more advantaged communities, while Government schools serve a wider range of communities including more disadvantaged ones.

Community advantage is not only a sector pattern. It is also geographic. Brushing across ICSEA scores shows where schools serving less advantaged communities are concentrated and how the mix of sectors changes across Australia.

That community advantage gap has a direct consequence for overseas families. The schools available to international students - those with CRICOS registration - are drawn almost entirely from the advantaged end of the scale.

Schools open to overseas students are concentrated in Australia's more advantaged communities

Not every Australian school is open to overseas students. To legally enrol, a school must hold CRICOS registration and the schools that have it do not reflect Australian schooling as a whole. Across all three sectors, CRICOS-registered schools sit noticeably higher on the community advantage scale than non-registered schools. The gap is largest in government schools, where the median registered school sits 28 percentile points above the median non-registered school. This shows that overseas families looking at public schooling can only choose from government schools in wealthier areas. Independent CRICOS schools sit even higher, with a median at the 92nd percentile. Whichever sector you choose, the schools available to you as an overseas family represent only the more advantaged end of Australian communities.

The geographic and international dimensions of advantage are clear. But when you look directly at the socio-economic backgrounds of students within each sector, the divide becomes even harder to ignore.

Size matters too. The largest schools in Australia are not evenly distributed across sectors or communities - and the gap in scale between the biggest and smallest schools reflects the same patterns of advantage this story has traced throughout.

How big are the largest schools?

The final part of the dashboard adds school size. Size does not mean quality, but it can affect the school experience because larger schools may have different facilities, subject choices, staffing needs and community structures.

Taken together, these patterns point to the same conclusion the opening question raised: in Australia, where you live and what your family earns still shapes which school you attend, what resources that school has, and who sits beside you in the classroom. The three sectors do not serve three equal slices of Australian society - they serve three different Australias. Understanding that gap is the first step toward addressing it.
